Agile & Scrum

Iterative development methodology for faster delivery and continuous improvement

Overview

Agile and Scrum represent a fundamental shift in how software is developed and projects are managed. Instead of traditional waterfall (plan everything upfront, then execute), Agile emphasizes iterative development, continuous feedback, and responsiveness to change. Scrum is the most popular Agile framework, providing roles, events, and artifacts that structure how teams work. Agile enables faster delivery, better quality, reduced risk, and higher customer satisfaction. It has become the standard in software development and is increasingly adopted for IT projects beyond development.

Core Principle: Agile values individuals and interactions over processes, working software over documentation, customer collaboration over contracts, and responding to change over following a plan.

Agile vs Waterfall

Waterfall Approach

  • Sequential phases: plan → design → build → test → deploy
  • Requirements defined upfront
  • Testing happens late in cycle
  • Change is difficult and expensive
  • Longer time to first delivery
  • Works for stable, well-defined projects

Agile Approach

  • Iterative: deliver working software frequently (weeks, not months)
  • Requirements emerge and evolve
  • Testing continuous throughout
  • Change embraced and encouraged
  • Early and continuous value delivery
  • Works for complex, evolving projects

Hybrid Approaches

  • Some projects use waterfall where appropriate (infrastructure, hardware)
  • Agile for custom development
  • Scrumfall for large programs with dependencies

Scrum Framework

Scrum Roles

  • Product Owner: Represents customer, prioritizes work, defines requirements
  • Scrum Master: Facilitates Scrum process, removes impediments, coaches team
  • Development Team: Self-organized, cross-functional group that delivers product

Scrum Events

  • Sprint Planning: Plan work for next sprint (2 hours per week of sprint)
  • Daily Standup: 15-minute daily check-in (what done, what doing, blockers)
  • Sprint Review: Demo completed work to stakeholders
  • Sprint Retrospective: Team reflects on process improvements

Scrum Artifacts

  • Product Backlog: Prioritized list of features and requirements
  • Sprint Backlog: Committed work for current sprint
  • Increment: Working product at end of sprint
  • Definition of Done: Criteria for work to be considered complete

Agile Principles

Customer Collaboration

  • Customer heavily involved in development
  • Regular feedback and validation
  • Early and continuous delivery of value
  • Changing requirements welcomed

Self-Organizing Teams

  • Teams determine how to accomplish work
  • Cross-functional with all needed skills
  • Individual accountability
  • Empowered decision-making

Continuous Improvement

  • Regular retrospectives to improve process
  • Quick adjustments based on feedback
  • Learning from failures and successes
  • Technical excellence focus (testing, refactoring)

Sustainable Pace

  • Avoid burnout through manageable workloads
  • Realistic velocity and commitments
  • Regular breaks and time off

Agile Metrics and Velocity

Sprint Velocity

  • Amount of work completed per sprint
  • Measured in story points
  • Used for forecasting and planning
  • Should stabilize after 3-4 sprints

Burndown Charts

  • Visual representation of work remaining
  • Track progress through sprint
  • Identify if sprint is on track

Cumulative Flow

  • Show work at different stages (to-do, in-progress, done)
  • Identify bottlenecks
  • Optimize flow

Release Planning Metrics

  • Story points completed per release
  • Release timeline projections
  • Quality metrics (defects, test coverage)

Scaling Agile

Multiple Team Coordination

  • Scrum of Scrums (representative from each team)
  • Shared backlogs for dependencies
  • Integration planning

SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework)

  • Coordinates multiple Agile teams
  • Portfolio, program, and team levels
  • Quarterly planning cycles

Large Program Challenges

  • Coordination complexity
  • Dependency management
  • Governance and reporting
  • Architectural consistency

Transitioning to Agile

Assessment

  • Evaluate organizational readiness
  • Identify teams suitable for Agile
  • Assess infrastructure and tools

Training and Coaching

  • Scrum and Agile training for teams
  • External coaching initially
  • Build internal Scrum Master capability

Pilot Programs

  • Start with one or two teams
  • Establish working practices
  • Learn and adjust
  • Expand gradually

Transformation Challenges

  • Resistance to change
  • Legacy processes and governance
  • Misaligned incentives
  • Skill gaps

Agile Tools

Popular Tools

  • Jira (atlassian) - widely used for Scrum
  • Azure DevOps - Microsoft's Agile tool
  • Trello - simpler Kanban approach
  • Monday.com - visual project management

Tool Functions

  • Product backlog management
  • Sprint planning and tracking
  • Burndown charts and reporting
  • Team collaboration
  • Integration with development tools

IT Director Responsibilities

  • Establish Agile as development standard
  • Invest in training and coaching
  • Select and implement Agile tools
  • Build Scrum Master and Product Owner capability
  • Establish Agile governance and metrics
  • Balance Agile with organizational governance
  • Support continuous improvement
  • Scale Agile for multiple teams
  • Measure Agile effectiveness
