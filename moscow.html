MoSCoW

MoSCoW – simple prioritization technique (Must have, Should have, Could have, Won't have)

Overview

MoSCoW is a widely used prioritization method that categorizes requirements, features, user stories, or initiatives into four groups: **Must have** (critical for success), **Should have** (important but not vital), **Could have** (desirable if time allows), and **Won't have** (out of scope for the current delivery). Originating from the DSDM (Dynamic Systems Development Method) agile framework, MoSCoW is valued for its simplicity, clarity, and effectiveness in stakeholder alignment. In March 2026, it remains extremely popular in agile, product management, digital transformation, and requirements workshops — often combined with **RICE**, **OKRs**, **value vs effort matrices**, and tools like **Jira**, **Azure DevOps**, **Productboard**, and **Aha!**. It is especially effective for time-boxed projects, MVP definition, backlog grooming, and managing stakeholder expectations when resources or timelines are constrained.

Key Value: MoSCoW provides a simple, collaborative, and transparent way to prioritize work — ensuring critical "Must have" items are delivered first, managing scope creep, facilitating stakeholder consensus, reducing ambiguity, and helping teams focus on delivering maximum value within time and resource constraints.

Core MoSCoW Categories (2026)

Must Have

  • Critical to project success or minimum viable product
  • Non-negotiable — project fails without them
  • Usually 60–70% of the total effort in a time-box

Should Have

  • Important but not critical
  • High business value, but workarounds exist
  • Delivered if time and resources allow

Could Have

  • Desirable enhancements or nice-to-haves
  • Low cost, high delight potential
  • Only included if time and budget permit

Won't Have (this time)

  • Explicitly out of scope for the current delivery
  • Helps manage expectations and prevent scope creep
  • Can be reconsidered in future phases

Key Trends & Best Practices in 2026

Hybrid & AI-Assisted Prioritization

  • Combining MoSCoW with RICE or OKRs
  • AI tools suggesting MoSCoW categories based on data
  • Regular re-prioritization during sprint planning

Stakeholder Collaboration

  • Facilitated workshops with clear definitions
  • Visual MoSCoW boards in Jira/Productboard
  • Documenting rationale for each category

Governance & Transparency

  • Explicit "Won't have" list to manage expectations
  • Tracking delivery against MoSCoW commitments
  • Post-delivery review of prioritization accuracy

Best Practices for MoSCoW Usage (2026)

Workshop & Definition

  • Clearly define "Must have" criteria with stakeholders
  • Use time-boxing to force trade-offs
  • Limit "Must have" to ~60–70% of capacity

Application & Review

  • Apply MoSCoW during backlog grooming and sprint planning
  • Revisit categories at the start of each release or phase
  • Track what was delivered vs original MoSCoW

Integration with Other Frameworks

  • Combine with RICE for quantitative scoring
  • Link "Must have" items to OKR key results
  • Use in conjunction with value vs effort matrices

IT Director / Product Responsibilities

  • Champion MoSCoW as transparent prioritization technique
  • Ensure consistent application across teams
  • Integrate MoSCoW with OKRs, RICE, and roadmap planning
  • Oversee prioritization accuracy and outcome tracking
  • Promote data-driven decisions over subjective opinions
  • Review and refine the MoSCoW approach periodically
  • Align prioritization with strategic, regulatory, and business goals
