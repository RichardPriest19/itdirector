WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines)

International standard for making web content accessible to people with disabilities – the global benchmark for digital accessibility

Overview

WCAG is developed by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C). It provides actionable success criteria and techniques to ensure web content is perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust for all users, including those with visual, auditory, motor, or cognitive impairments.

Current Version (2026)

WCAG 2.2 is the latest stable recommendation. WCAG 2.1 remains widely referenced for legal compliance. WCAG 3.0 (project name “Silver”) is in development with a new scoring model.

Four Core Principles (POUR)

  • Perceivable – Information and UI components must be presentable to users in ways they can perceive
  • Operable – User interface components and navigation must be operable
  • Understandable – Information and operation of the UI must be understandable
  • Robust – Content must be robust enough to be interpreted reliably by a wide variety of user agents, including assistive technologies

Conformance Levels

  • Level A – Minimum level of accessibility
  • Level AA – The most commonly targeted level for public sector and enterprise compliance (recommended standard)
  • Level AAA – Highest level of accessibility (not always fully achievable)

Relevance for IT Directors in 2026

WCAG compliance is now a legal requirement in many jurisdictions (e.g., EU Accessibility Act, UK Public Sector Bodies Regulations, US Section 508). Non-compliance carries significant legal, reputational, and financial risk. IT leaders must ensure all digital properties — websites, intranets, portals, applications, and documents — meet at least WCAG 2.1/2.2 Level AA.
