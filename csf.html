CSF

NIST Cybersecurity Framework – voluntary framework for managing cyber risk

Overview

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) is a voluntary, risk-based framework developed by the U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) to help organizations manage and reduce cybersecurity risk. In March 2026, **CSF 2.0** (released February 2024) is the most widely adopted cybersecurity framework globally — used by enterprises, critical infrastructure operators, government agencies, supply-chain partners, and even non-U.S. organizations as a harmonized reference. It consists of six core **Functions** (Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover), with new emphasis on **Govern** as a foundational function, **cyber supply chain risk management (C-SCRM)**, **AI/ML risk integration**, **expanded profiles** (manufacturing, healthcare, small business, AI-specific), and **regulatory mapping** (DORA, NIS2, SEC 4-hour disclosure, CMMC 2.0, HIPAA, PCI-DSS). CSF 2.0 is outcome-focused, flexible, and widely accepted for **board reporting**, **cyber insurance underwriting**, **zero-trust alignment**, **SEC disclosure justification**, and **supply-chain due diligence** (EO 14028 flow-down).

Key Value: CSF 2.0 provides a clear, flexible, outcome-based structure for managing cyber risk — enabling prioritization, communication with non-technical stakeholders, alignment with regulations & insurance requirements, supply-chain risk management, AI/ML governance, and — when mapped to maturity models — measurable progress toward resilience and defensible cybersecurity posture.

Core Functions (CSF 2.0 – 2026)

Govern (New in 2.0)

  • Cybersecurity strategy, roles, oversight & policy
  • Board & executive accountability
  • Risk management strategy & supply-chain governance

Identify

  • Asset management, risk assessment, supply-chain risk
  • Business context & improvement planning
  • AI/ML system inventory & risk classification

Protect

  • Identity management, data security, platform security
  • Resilient infrastructure & awareness training
  • Secure software development & supply-chain practices

Detect, Respond, Recover

  • Continuous monitoring, anomaly detection, incident response
  • Communications, analysis, mitigation, recovery planning
  • Post-incident lessons learned & improvements

Key Trends & Best Practices in 2026

Regulatory & Insurance Alignment

  • CSF 2.0 as baseline for SEC disclosure justification
  • Mapping to DORA, NIS2, CMMC 2.0, HIPAA
  • Cyber insurance underwriting & premium reduction

AI & Emerging Risk

  • AI Profile addendum for generative AI risk management
  • Govern function for AI governance & accountability
  • Supply-chain risk for AI/ML models & datasets

Supply-Chain & Zero Trust

  • C-SCRM integration in Identify & Govern
  • Zero-trust alignment with Protect & Detect
  • Third-party risk scoring & contractual flow-down

Best Practices for CSF Adoption (2026)

Implementation & Mapping

  • Start with current profile & gap analysis
  • Map CSF to existing controls (NIST 800-53, ISO 27001)
  • Prioritize Govern & C-SCRM functions

Governance & Reporting

  • Integrate CSF into board & executive reporting
  • Use CSF profiles for maturity scoring
  • Align with cyber insurance & regulatory requirements

Continuous Improvement

  • Conduct annual CSF reviews & maturity assessments
  • Integrate with risk registers & incident lessons learned
  • Update for AI/ML & emerging risks

IT Director / CISO Responsibilities

  • Champion CSF 2.0 as enterprise cyber risk framework
  • Approve CSF adoption roadmap & governance model
  • Ensure C-SCRM, AI risk & third-party oversight
  • Oversee maturity scoring, gap closure & reporting
  • Monitor CSF alignment with insurance & regulations
  • Drive integration with zero-trust & incident management
  • Align CSF with board, ESG & strategic priorities
