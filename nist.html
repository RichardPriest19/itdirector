NIST Cybersecurity Framework

National Institute of Standards and Technology cybersecurity guidance and best practices

Overview

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) is a voluntary, flexible framework created by the U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology. It provides standards, guidelines, and best practices to manage cybersecurity risks. Originally developed in 2014 for critical infrastructure protection, it has become widely adopted across all sectors and industries worldwide. Unlike ISO 27001, NIST is not certifiable, but organizations use it to strengthen their security posture and manage cyber risks. NIST CSF is particularly valued for its flexibility, risk-based approach, and integration with organizational objectives.

Key Distinction: NIST CSF is a framework (not certifiable), but many organizations use it alongside ISO 27001 to provide strategic direction while ISO 27001 provides certified implementation.

NIST CSF History and Development

NIST Cybersecurity Framework v1.0 (2014)

Original framework developed through collaboration between government and industry. Focused on critical infrastructure protection. Organized around five core functions.

NIST CSF v1.1 (2018)

Minor update and clarifications. Aligned with supply chain risk management requirements.

NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 (2024)

Major revision emphasizing supply chain risk, third-party threats, and governance. Enhanced integration with enterprise risk management. Greater focus on managing threats and securing against threats. Refined categories and outcomes.

The Five Core Functions

NIST CSF is organized around five core functions that provide strategic organization and structure:

1. GOVERN

Establish organizational context and governance structure for cybersecurity. New in CSF 2.0, elevating governance to primary level.

  • Define organizational cybersecurity strategy aligned with business
  • Establish governance roles and responsibilities
  • Integrate cybersecurity with enterprise risk management
  • Manage risks from suppliers and third parties
  • Develop policies and implement controls
  • Monitor and oversee cybersecurity

2. IDENTIFY

Develop organizational understanding of cybersecurity risk.

  • Asset management: Understand what needs to be protected
  • Business environment: Understand mission and operations
  • Governance and risk strategy: Understand strategy and risk tolerance
  • Risk assessment: Assess organizational cybersecurity risks
  • Risk management strategy: Develop approach to managing identified risks

3. PROTECT

Implement safeguards to ensure delivery of critical services.

  • Access control: Restrict access to systems and data
  • Asset management: Maintain inventory and status of systems
  • Business continuity and resilience: Plan for disruptions
  • Data security: Protect confidentiality and integrity
  • Information protection processes: Manage protection of information
  • Protective technology: Deploy security technology
  • Supply chain security: Manage supplier security

4. DETECT

Implement monitoring and detection capabilities to identify cybersecurity incidents.

  • Anomalies and events: Identify unexpected events or behavior
  • Continuous monitoring: Maintain constant monitoring of systems
  • Detection processes: Implement processes to identify incidents

5. RESPOND

Implement processes to address cybersecurity incidents when detected.

  • Incident response planning: Prepare response procedures
  • Communications and analysis: Analyze and communicate about incidents
  • Mitigation and improvement: Stop incident impact and improve

6. RECOVER

Implement processes to restore normal operations after incidents.

  • Recovery planning: Plan for service restoration
  • Recovery communications: Communicate during recovery
  • Recovery improvements: Learn from incidents and improve

Note: NIST CSF 1.x had 5 functions (Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover). CSF 2.0 added GOVERN as primary function, elevating governance importance.

NIST CSF Categories and Subcategories

Framework Structure

Each of the six core functions contains multiple categories, which are further broken down into subcategories:

  • Functions: 6 strategic business capabilities (GOVERN, IDENTIFY, PROTECT, DETECT, RESPOND, RECOVER)
  • Categories: Approximately 22 categories describing specific outcomes
  • Subcategories: Over 200 specific outcomes that organizations can achieve

Outcome-Focused Approach

Rather than prescribing specific controls, NIST describes outcomes. Organizations determine how to achieve outcomes using appropriate controls and technologies.

NIST CSF Implementation Profiles

Profiling Concept

NIST allows organizations to develop implementation profiles based on their specific business needs and risk tolerance:

Current Profile

Describes the current state of the organization's cybersecurity practices. Assessment of what is currently being done. Baseline for comparison.

Target Profile

Describes the desired future state. Based on business requirements, regulatory requirements, and risk assessment. Represents what the organization wants to achieve.

Gap Analysis

Identifies differences between current and target profiles. Drives improvement efforts and resource allocation. Prioritizes improvements based on business impact and risk.

Implementation Roadmap

Developed from gap analysis. Specifies activities, resources, and timelines to move from current to target state. Includes quick wins and strategic improvements.

NIST vs ISO 27001 vs ITIL

NIST Cybersecurity Framework

  • Type: Framework (non-certifiable)
  • Focus: Cybersecurity risk management
  • Approach: Flexible, outcome-based guidance
  • Best for: Strategic risk-based approach, government contractors

ISO 27001

  • Type: Standard (certifiable)
  • Focus: Information security management system
  • Approach: Systematic, requirements-based
  • Best for: Third-party credibility, compliance demonstrations

ITIL

  • Type: Framework (certifiable certifications)
  • Focus: IT Service Management
  • Approach: Process-based best practices
  • Best for: Service delivery alignment with business

Complementary Use

Many organizations use all three: NIST for strategic framework, ISO 27001 for certified ISMS implementation, ITIL for service management processes. They address different dimensions of IT governance.

NIST CSF 2.0 Key Changes

New GOVERN Function

Elevates governance to primary level, separate from IDENTIFY. Recognizes that governance is foundational to all other functions.

Supply Chain Focus

Enhanced emphasis on managing risks from suppliers, vendors, and third parties. Reflects reality of interconnected organizations.

Threat Management

Greater focus on managing threats and threats actors. More explicit language about threat assessment and response.

Enterprise Risk Integration

Stronger integration with enterprise risk management and business continuity. Cybersecurity framed as part of broader risk management.

Refined Outcomes

More specific, measurable outcomes. Clearer guidance on what organizations should accomplish.

Governance Across Functions

Governance principles embedded throughout all functions, not just in GOVERN. Recognition that governance is cross-cutting.

NIST Special Publications (SP) Supporting Documents

NIST SP 800 Series

Technical guidance publications supporting NIST CSF implementation:

  • SP 800-12: Introduction to Computer Security
  • SP 800-30: Risk Assessment guidance
  • SP 800-39: Managing Information and Technology Risk
  • SP 800-53: Security and Privacy Controls
  • SP 800-61: Incident Handling guidance
  • SP 800-82: Industrial Control Systems Security
  • SP 800-171: Protecting Controlled Unclassified Information

NIST SP 800-207

Zero Trust Architecture—emerging security model for the modern threat landscape.

NIST CSF Benefits

  • Flexible framework adaptable to any organization size, industry, or risk profile
  • Risk-based approach focuses resources on what matters most
  • Common language for cybersecurity across organization and with external partners
  • Outcome-focused rather than prescriptive, allowing flexibility in implementation
  • Improves communication with board and senior management
  • Supports regulatory compliance efforts across multiple requirements
  • Excellent starting point for organizations beginning cybersecurity journey
  • Widely recognized and accepted by government and industry
  • Aligns cybersecurity with business objectives and strategy
  • Supports continuous improvement and risk management maturity

Who Should Use NIST CSF

Government Contractors

Often required by government contracts. Critical for defense and federal IT projects.

Critical Infrastructure Operators

Strongly recommended for utilities, energy, financial, healthcare, communications.

Organizations in Regulated Industries

Financial services, healthcare, government agencies, telecommunications.

Organizations Pursuing Cybersecurity Excellence

Any organization wanting risk-based, strategic approach to cybersecurity.

Startup and Growing Companies

Flexible framework allows scaling as organization grows.

NIST CSF Implementation Steps

Phase 1: Prepare

  • Understand organizational priorities and risk tolerance
  • Gain executive sponsorship and commitment
  • Build team with cross-functional expertise
  • Learn NIST CSF framework and concepts

Phase 2: Assess Current State

  • Document current cybersecurity practices
  • Map to NIST CSF functions and categories
  • Identify gaps and weaknesses
  • Develop current profile

Phase 3: Define Target State

  • Determine target cybersecurity maturity level
  • Align with business objectives and risk tolerance
  • Develop target profile
  • Prioritize gaps for remediation

Phase 4: Plan and Implement

  • Develop implementation roadmap with timelines
  • Allocate resources and budget
  • Implement controls and improvements
  • Track progress and adjust as needed

Phase 5: Monitor and Improve

  • Continuously monitor cybersecurity posture
  • Measure effectiveness of controls
  • Identify areas for improvement
  • Update profiles as threat landscape changes

Aligning NIST CSF with Business

Strategic Alignment

NIST CSF can be aligned with business strategy:

  • Map business objectives to NIST CSF outcomes
  • Link cybersecurity investments to business value
  • Demonstrate ROI of security initiatives
  • Communicate risk in business terms

Risk-Based Decision Making

Use NIST CSF to:

  • Prioritize investments based on risk impact
  • Make informed accept/mitigate/avoid decisions
  • Balance security with business needs
  • Demonstrate due diligence to stakeholders

NIST CSF and Supply Chain Risk Management

Third-Party Risk Management

NIST CSF 2.0 emphasizes supply chain security:

  • Assess cybersecurity of suppliers and vendors
  • Establish security requirements in contracts
  • Monitor compliance with security requirements
  • Plan response to supplier incidents
  • Manage dependencies on third-party systems

NIST CSF Limitations and Considerations

Not Prescriptive

Organizations must determine how to implement—requires expertise and decision-making.

No Certification

No third-party certification. Organizations cannot claim NIST CSF certification (though some vendors market this).

Implementation Effort

Requires significant effort to properly understand, implement, and maintain.

Resource Intensive

Small organizations may find profiling and implementation resource-intensive.

IT Director Responsibilities in NIST CSF

  • Lead assessment of current cybersecurity posture against NIST CSF
  • Develop current state profile identifying existing controls
  • Define target profile aligned with business and risk tolerance
  • Implement technical controls across all functions
  • Establish monitoring and detection capabilities
  • Develop incident response and recovery procedures
  • Assess and manage risks from suppliers and third parties
  • Report cybersecurity posture to executive management
  • Prioritize improvements based on risk and business value
  • Maintain continuous improvement and measurement

NIST CSF vs Compliance Frameworks

Complementary, Not Competitive

NIST CSF can be used alongside compliance frameworks:

  • GDPR: Use NIST CSF for overall governance; GDPR for specific privacy requirements
  • PCI-DSS: Use NIST CSF for risk management; PCI-DSS for specific payment card security
  • HIPAA: Use NIST CSF for healthcare security governance; HIPAA for specific healthcare privacy
  • SOC 2: NIST CSF provides framework; SOC 2 provides audit/attestation

Many organizations map compliance requirements to NIST CSF to ensure comprehensive coverage.

Measuring NIST CSF Implementation

Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)

Measure effectiveness of NIST CSF implementation:

  • Percentage of functions at target maturity
  • Number and severity of unmitigated risks
  • Incident detection and response time
  • Control compliance percentage
  • Security training completion rates

Maturity Assessment

Many organizations use maturity models to assess progress. Stages typically include:

  • Partial: Initial awareness and some controls
  • Repeatable: Documented processes and procedures
  • Defined: Integrated with business processes
  • Managed: Quantitatively managed and measured
  • Optimized: Continuously improved
