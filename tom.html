TOM

Target Operating Model – blueprint for how an organization will operate to deliver its strategy

Overview

A Target Operating Model (TOM) is a high-level blueprint that defines how an organization will operate in the future to deliver its strategy, vision, and objectives. It describes the desired future state across people, processes, technology, governance, data, culture, and organizational structure. In March 2026, TOMs are widely used during digital transformation, cloud migrations, ERP implementations (SAP, Oracle, IFS, Xledger), post-merger integration, shared services creation, and cost-optimization programs. A well-designed TOM aligns operating model elements with strategic goals, enables effective change management, reduces implementation risk, and provides a clear reference point for decision-making across IT, finance, HR, and business units.

Key Value: A clear TOM bridges strategy and execution — creating alignment, reducing ambiguity, guiding technology investments, enabling efficient operating model design, supporting successful transformations, and providing a shared vision that helps leadership and teams make consistent, value-driven decisions.

Core TOM Components (2026)

Strategy & Value

  • Strategic objectives, vision, and value drivers
  • Target customer segments and value propositions
  • Key performance outcomes and success metrics

Operating Model Layers

  • Processes & workflows (end-to-end value streams)
  • Organization structure, roles & responsibilities (RACI)
  • Technology & systems architecture
  • Governance, controls & decision rights

Enablers

  • Data, analytics & AI capabilities
  • Culture, skills & change management
  • Locations, facilities & ecosystem partnerships

Key Trends & Best Practices in 2026

Digital & AI-First TOM

  • AI-augmented processes and decision automation
  • Composable & modular operating models
  • Hyper-automation and low-code integration

Agile & Hybrid Operating Models

  • Product-centric vs project-centric structures
  • Platform operating models & shared services
  • Hybrid agile + traditional delivery

Governance & Sustainability

  • ESG integration into operating model design
  • Clear decision rights and accountability matrices
  • Continuous TOM review and evolution

Best Practices for TOM Development (2026)

Design Process

  • Start with strategy and value drivers
  • Use workshops with cross-functional stakeholders
  • Define current state vs target state gap analysis

Key Deliverables

  • High-level TOM diagram and operating principles
  • RACI matrix and capability map
  • Technology & data architecture overview

Implementation

  • Phase the transition (quick wins + long-term changes)
  • Align change management and training programs
  • Establish governance for ongoing TOM evolution

IT Director Responsibilities

  • Lead the technology and data aspects of the TOM
  • Ensure alignment between business strategy and IT capabilities
  • Drive composable architecture and low-code enablement
  • Oversee integration with ERP, CRM, and cloud platforms
  • Monitor TOM realization, benefits tracking & adjustments
  • Align TOM with cybersecurity, compliance & ESG goals
  • Support change management and cultural transformation
