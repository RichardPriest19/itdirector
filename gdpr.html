GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

EU regulation on data protection and privacy rights for individuals

Overview

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) is a comprehensive data protection law enacted by the European Union that became enforceable on May 25, 2018. It establishes rights for individuals and obligations for organizations regarding personal data. GDPR applies globally to any organization handling personal data of EU residents, not just those based in the EU. It represents the most stringent data privacy regulation in the world and has inspired similar regulations worldwide.

Critical: Non-compliance with GDPR can result in administrative fines of up to €20 million or 4% of annual global revenue (whichever is higher) for the most serious violations. Lesser violations can result in fines up to €10 million or 2% of revenue.

GDPR Scope and Applicability

Who Must Comply?

  • Any organization in the EU: All businesses and organizations based in EU member states
  • Organizations outside EU targeting EU residents: Organizations offering goods or services to EU residents
  • Organizations monitoring EU residents: Organizations tracking behavior or activities of EU residents
  • Data processors: Organizations processing data on behalf of others
  • Third-party service providers: Organizations handling personal data of EU residents

What is Personal Data?

Any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person. Includes:

  • Names and identification numbers (ID cards, passport, social security)
  • Location data (GPS, IP addresses, cell tower triangulation)
  • Online identifiers (usernames, cookies, device IDs)
  • Physical characteristics (fingerprints, face recognition data)
  • Genetic data (DNA sequences)
  • Email addresses and phone numbers
  • Employment and financial information
  • Health information and medical records

Special Categories of Data (Sensitive Data)

Extra protection applies to sensitive data:

  • Racial or ethnic origin: Protected against discrimination
  • Political opinions: Protected beliefs and affiliations
  • Religious or philosophical beliefs: Protected personal convictions
  • Trade union membership: Protected organizational affiliations
  • Genetic data: DNA and hereditary information
  • Biometric data: Fingerprints, facial recognition, iris scans for identification
  • Health data: Medical history, test results, diagnoses
  • Data concerning sex life or sexual orientation: Protected personal information

Core GDPR Principles

1. Lawfulness, Fairness, and Transparency

Personal data processing must be legal and transparent. Organizations must provide clear information about data processing to individuals. Processing must be fair and not cause unjustified harm.

2. Purpose Limitation

Data collected for specific purposes cannot be reused for incompatible purposes without additional legal basis. Limits mission creep and secondary use of personal data.

3. Data Minimization

Collect only personal data that is necessary for the stated purpose. Avoid collecting excessive data. Principle of least privilege applies.

4. Accuracy

Personal data must be accurate, complete, and kept up to date. Organizations must correct inaccurate data. Individuals have right to rectification.

5. Storage Limitation

Keep personal data only as long as necessary for the purpose. Implement data retention schedules. Delete or anonymize data when no longer needed.

6. Integrity and Confidentiality

Data must be secure and protected against unauthorized processing, accidental loss, destruction, or damage. Requires appropriate technical and organizational security measures.

7. Accountability

Organizations must demonstrate compliance with GDPR. Maintain documentation and evidence of compliance. Implement governance and controls.

Legal Basis for Processing

Organizations must have at least one lawful basis to process personal data. Cannot process without a valid basis:

1. Consent

The individual has given clear, informed, freely-given, specific, and unambiguous consent. Consent must be optional and granular. Silence or inaction is not consent.

2. Contract

Processing is necessary to perform a contract with the individual or take pre-contractual steps at their request. Processing is essential for the relationship.

3. Legal Obligation

Processing is required by law. Organization must comply with legal requirements (tax, employment, regulatory).

4. Vital Interests

Processing is necessary to protect vital interests of a person (life or health). Typically used in emergency or medical situations.

5. Public Task

Processing is necessary for public function or official authority. Typically applies to government and public sector organizations.

6. Legitimate Interests

Organization's legitimate interests outweigh individual's rights and freedoms. Must conduct Balancing Test (Legitimate Interest Assessment or LIA). Not available for public authorities.

Individual Rights Under GDPR

Right of Access

Individuals have right to obtain confirmation of whether personal data is being processed and to access that data. Organizations must provide copy of personal data in structured format. Must respond within 30 days.

Right to Rectification

Individuals can correct, update, or complete inaccurate personal data. Organization must correct without undue delay. Must inform third parties of correction if requested.

Right to Erasure ("Right to be Forgotten")

Delete personal data under certain conditions: no longer necessary, consent withdrawn, no lawful basis, or legal obligation to delete. Exceptions: legal obligation to retain, freedom of expression, public interest, or legitimate interests.

Right to Restrict Processing

Limit how organization uses personal data. Individual can request processing be restricted while accuracy is disputed, legality is being determined, or rights are being determined. Still can store data, cannot use without consent.

Right to Data Portability

Receive personal data in structured, commonly-used format (CSV, XML, JSON) and transfer to another controller. Applies only when processing is based on consent or contract and processing is automated.

Right to Object

Object to processing at any time. Processing must stop unless compelling legitimate reasons exist. Must always have right to object to marketing. Automatic right to object to profiling.

Rights Related to Automated Decision-Making

Not be subject to decisions based solely on automated processing (profiling, behavioral analysis) that produce legal effects or similarly significant effects. Right to human review and explanation.

Right to Withdraw Consent

Withdraw consent at any time. Withdrawal is as easy as giving consent. Past processing based on consent is lawful.

Key GDPR Compliance Requirements

Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)

Required for high-risk processing activities. Must identify risks to individuals and implement mitigation measures. Mandatory for:

  • Large-scale processing of personal data
  • Systematic monitoring of individuals
  • Automated decision-making with legal effects
  • Processing of sensitive data on large scale
  • Use of new technologies

Data Protection Officer (DPO)

Must appoint a DPO if:

  • Public authority or body
  • Organization whose core activities involve regular, systematic monitoring of individuals
  • Organization whose core activities involve processing of special categories of data at scale

DPO responsibilities: oversight of compliance, point of contact for individuals and authorities, conduct audits, maintain documentation, report to executive management.

Privacy by Design and Default

Integrate data protection principles into all processing activities from the outset, not as an afterthought. Default settings must provide highest level of protection. Implement appropriate technical and organizational measures.

Data Breach Notification

Upon becoming aware of a personal data breach:

  • Notify supervisory authority within 72 hours (with exceptions)
  • Document the breach (date, facts, effects, remedial actions)
  • Notify affected individuals without undue delay if high risk to rights and freedoms
  • Inform relevant organizations and media if necessary

Privacy Notices

Provide clear, transparent information when collecting personal data:

  • Identity of data controller
  • Purpose of processing
  • Legal basis for processing
  • Recipients of data
  • Retention period
  • Individual rights
  • Complaint procedures

Data Processing Agreements (DPA)

Written contracts required when engaging third-party processors or sharing data with other organizations. Must specify:

  • Scope and nature of processing
  • Purpose of processing
  • Type of data and individuals
  • Security obligations
  • Sub-processor management
  • Individual rights support

Records of Processing Activities

Maintain documentation of all data processing activities:

  • Name and contact of organization
  • Name and contact of DPO (if applicable)
  • Purposes of processing
  • Data categories and individual categories
  • Recipients of data
  • Retention periods
  • Security measures

Consent Management

If using consent as legal basis:

  • Obtain prior consent before processing
  • Consent must be freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous
  • Separate consent for different purposes
  • Easy withdrawal mechanism
  • Maintain records of consent

International Data Transfers

Transferring personal data outside the EU/EEA requires adequate safeguards. Current mechanisms include:

Adequacy Decisions

EU Commission has determined some countries have adequate protection (Japan, Canada, South Korea, Switzerland, UK, Israel). Transfers to these countries are unrestricted.

Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)

Contracts with approved wording that ensure adequate protections when transferring to non-adequate countries. Recently updated (2021). Must be supplemented with additional measures in some cases (post-Schrems II ruling).

Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs)

Internal transfer mechanism for multinational organizations. Sets consistent rules across group entities. Requires approval from supervisory authorities.

Supplementary Measures (Post-Schrems II)

After Schrems II ruling, organizations must assess third-country laws and implement supplementary measures (encryption, anonymization, contractual commitments) if government access is possible.

GDPR Enforcement and Penalties

Supervisory Authorities

Each EU member state has a Data Protection Authority (DPA) responsible for enforcement. In EU, there is also European Data Protection Board (EDPB). Individuals can file complaints with DPA of their member state.

Administrative Fines

  • Up to €20 million or 4% of global revenue: Most serious violations (core principles, individual rights, international transfers)
  • Up to €10 million or 2% of global revenue: Other violations (technical/organizational measures, DPO requirements, security breaches)

Private Right of Action

Individuals can sue organizations for damages caused by GDPR violations. Liability can apply even without administrative fine.

Supervisory Authority Powers

  • Issue warnings and reprimands
  • Impose administrative fines
  • Restrict processing
  • Suspend data transfer authorization
  • Require corrective action

IT Director Responsibilities in GDPR Compliance

  • Ensure IT systems are designed with privacy and security in mind from inception
  • Implement appropriate technical and organizational security measures
  • Implement encryption for data in transit and at rest
  • Maintain comprehensive data inventory and processing records
  • Establish data breach detection and response procedures
  • Implement access controls and authentication mechanisms
  • Conduct regular security audits and vulnerability assessments
  • Ensure staff training on GDPR and data protection
  • Work closely with Data Protection Officer and Legal teams
  • Manage third-party and vendor data protection compliance
  • Maintain compliance documentation and evidence
  • Plan data retention and secure deletion procedures
  • Implement data subject rights support systems (access, deletion, portability)

Common GDPR Pitfalls

  • Collecting data without clear lawful basis or transparency
  • Not obtaining explicit consent where required
  • Failing to respond to data subject access requests within 30 days
  • Inadequate technical and organizational security measures
  • Not conducting DPIAs for high-risk processing
  • Sharing data with third parties without proper agreements
  • Retaining data longer than necessary
  • Not notifying breaches to authority within 72 hours
  • Insufficient privacy notices and transparency
  • Transferring data internationally without safeguards
  • Assuming consent covers all processing purposes
  • Not implementing data portability functionality

GDPR Compliance Roadmap

Phase 1: Assessment (Weeks 1-4)

  • Identify all data processing activities
  • Map data flows and storage locations
  • Assess current security and technical measures
  • Identify compliance gaps

Phase 2: Governance Setup (Weeks 5-8)

  • Appoint Data Protection Officer if required
  • Establish data protection policies and procedures
  • Conduct DPIAs for high-risk processing
  • Create privacy notices and templates

Phase 3: Technical Implementation (Months 3-6)

  • Implement encryption and security measures
  • Build data subject rights capabilities
  • Implement data retention and deletion procedures
  • Configure access controls and monitoring

Phase 4: Testing and Deployment (Months 6-12)

  • Test data subject request fulfillment
  • Test breach notification procedures
  • Conduct staff training
  • Deploy privacy measures across organization
